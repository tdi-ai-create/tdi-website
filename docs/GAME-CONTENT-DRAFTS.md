## 1. Sort It Out -- Full Scenario Sets

### Scenario 1: Feedback or Praise?
**Instruction:** Drag each statement into the correct bucket. Is it actionable feedback or feel-good praise?

| Statement | Correct Bucket | Why |
|-----------|---------------|-----|
| Good job! | Praise | No specifics. Student cannot replicate what they did. |
| I notice you used three details about the setting. That is descriptive writing. Now try adding what the character hears. | Feedback | Notice + Name + Next Step. All three parts. |
| You are so smart! | Praise | Labels the person, not the behavior. Fixed mindset. |
| Your handwriting is improving. Keep it up! | Praise | Mentions skill but no specific example or next step. |
| I see you lined up the decimal points. That is called alignment. Now explain to your partner why it matters. | Feedback | Specific + Named + Actionable + Extension. |
| Nice work today! | Praise | Which work? What was nice about it? |
| You broke this problem into two steps. That is decomposing. Can you explain why you split it there? | Feedback | All three parts plus metacognitive extension. |
| Almost there! | Praise | Almost where? No path forward. |
| I notice you asked a clarifying question before starting. That is active listening. How did that change your approach? | Feedback | Notice + Name + Reflection prompt. |
| Love it! | Praise | Teaches nothing. Cannot be repeated. |

### Scenario 2: Proactive or Reactive Classroom Management?
**Instruction:** Is this strategy preventing problems before they happen, or responding after they happen?

| Strategy | Correct Bucket | Why |
|----------|---------------|-----|
| Greeting students at the door by name | Proactive | Sets tone, builds connection before class starts. |
| Sending a student to the office after a blow-up | Reactive | Responding to a crisis that already happened. |
| Teaching and practicing routines the first two weeks | Proactive | Investment now saves hundreds of redirections later. |
| Taking away recess for not finishing work | Reactive | Punishing after the fact instead of preventing the problem. |
| Strategic seating chart based on student needs | Proactive | Prevents conflicts and distractions before they start. |
| Calling a parent after a bad day | Reactive | Important but responding to something that already happened. |
| Posted visual schedule that students can reference | Proactive | Reduces "what are we doing?" questions before they happen. |
| Raising your voice to get the room quiet | Reactive | Responding to noise instead of having a system for transitions. |
| Establishing a calm-down corner with tools | Proactive | System in place before dysregulation happens. |
| Writing a behavior referral | Reactive | Documentation of something that already occurred. |

### Scenario 3: Accommodation or Modification?
**Instruction:** Same content with different access (accommodation) or different content entirely (modification)?

| Support | Correct Bucket | Why |
|---------|---------------|-----|
| Extended time on a test | Accommodation | Same test, more time to access it. |
| Fewer questions on a test | Modification | Different assessment, reduced content expectations. |
| Text-to-speech software for reading assignments | Accommodation | Same content, different way to access it. |
| Alternative assignment on a different topic | Modification | Entirely different content and expectations. |
| Preferential seating near the teacher | Accommodation | Same lesson, better position to access it. |
| Simplified vocabulary in word problems | Modification | Changed the content itself to reduce language demand. |
| Graphic organizer for essay writing | Accommodation | Same writing task, structured support to access it. |
| Grade-level reading replaced with below-grade text | Modification | Different content level entirely. |
| Frequent check-ins during independent work | Accommodation | Same work, additional support to stay on track. |
| Pass/fail grading instead of letter grade | Modification | Different evaluation criteria for the same work. |

### Scenario 4: Growth Mindset or Fixed Mindset Language?
**Instruction:** Would Carol Dweck call this growth or fixed?

| Statement | Correct Bucket | Why |
|-----------|---------------|-----|
| You worked really hard on this. | Growth | Praises effort, not innate ability. |
| You are a natural at math. | Fixed | Implies ability is innate, not developed. |
| What strategy did you use? | Growth | Focuses on process, not outcome. |
| Some kids just get it and some do not. | Fixed | Implies ability is unchangeable. |
| This is challenging, but you are building the skill. | Growth | Normalizes struggle as part of learning. |
| You are not a math person. | Fixed | Labels the person as incapable. |
| Your brain is growing every time you practice this. | Growth | Frames effort as building capacity. |
| If you do not get it by now, you probably never will. | Fixed | Declares a ceiling on learning. |
| What would you do differently next time? | Growth | Assumes there will be a next time and improvement is possible. |
| You either have it or you do not. | Fixed | Binary thinking about ability. |

### Scenario 5: Your Responsibility or Not? (Para Edition)
**Instruction:** As a paraprofessional, is this your job, the teacher's job, or shared?

| Task | Correct Bucket | Why |
|------|---------------|-----|
| Writing lesson plans | Teacher | Paras support instruction, they do not design it. |
| Reinforcing a skill with a small group | Para | Core para function: targeted support. |
| Deciding a student's grade | Teacher | Grading is a teacher responsibility. |
| Collecting data on student behavior | Shared | Both observe, but the teacher interprets and decides. |
| Communicating with parents about academics | Teacher | Teacher owns the parent relationship for academic content. |
| Helping a student with daily living skills | Para | Direct student support is core para work. |
| Modifying materials for a student with an IEP | Shared | Teacher decides the modification, para may help create it. |
| Attending IEP meetings | Shared | Both perspectives are valuable. Para provides daily observation data. |
| Managing classroom behavior for the whole class | Teacher | Whole-class management is the teacher's responsibility. |
| Redirecting a student during independent work | Para | In-the-moment support during instruction. |

## 2. Parent Communication -- This or That Scenarios

### Scenario 1
**Context:** A parent emails you at 9 PM on a Sunday night asking about their child's grade. The tone is anxious, not angry.
- **A:** Respond briefly that evening: "Got your message. I will pull the details and get back to you by Tuesday." *(Acknowledge fast)*
- **B:** Wait until Monday morning during work hours to respond with a full, detailed reply. *(Protect your boundary)*
- **Seed data:** 45% A / 55% B
- **Research:** Work-life boundary research (Maslach burnout). Brief acknowledgment takes 30 seconds and reduces parent anxiety without sacrificing your evening.

### Scenario 2
**Context:** During a conference, a parent says "My child says you yell at the class." You have raised your voice twice this year, both times during safety situations.
- **A:** Acknowledge their concern first: "Thank you for bringing that to me. That is not the environment I want either. Let me share what happened." *(Validate then explain)*
- **B:** Clarify immediately: "I want to give you the full context. Those two moments were safety situations where I needed the room's attention instantly." *(Context first)*
- **Seed data:** 62% A / 38% B
- **Research:** Nonviolent Communication (Rosenberg). Leading with acknowledgment builds trust. Leading with defense builds walls.

### Scenario 3
**Context:** A parent volunteers to help in your classroom but keeps taking over activities and redirecting students in ways that conflict with your approach.
- **A:** Have a private conversation: "I love having you here. Can we talk about how to make the most of your time so it supports what we are working on?" *(Direct but warm)*
- **B:** Give them a specific, defined role: "Today I would love for you to listen to these 4 students read. Here is exactly what to listen for." *(Structure the role)*
- **Seed data:** 40% A / 60% B
- **Research:** Volunteer management (Epstein). Clear role definition prevents overstepping without an awkward conversation.

### Scenario 4
**Context:** You need to tell a parent their child is struggling significantly. The parent has expressed in the past that their child is "gifted."
- **A:** Lead with specific strengths first, then name the gap with data: "Here is where [student] shines. And here is where I see an opportunity to grow, based on these specific assessments." *(Strengths then gaps)*
- **B:** Be direct and compassionate: "I care about [student] and I want to be honest with you. The data shows [specific area] needs targeted support. Here is my plan." *(Honest and forward-looking)*
- **Seed data:** 58% A / 42% B
- **Research:** Difficult conversations framework (Stone, Patton, Heen). Both approaches work. A builds the relationship runway. B respects the parent's intelligence.

### Scenario 5
**Context:** A parent sends a group text to other parents criticizing a classroom policy you set. Another parent forwards it to you.
- **A:** Reach out directly to the parent: "I heard there are some concerns about [policy]. I would love to talk about it and hear your perspective." *(Address directly)*
- **B:** Send a class-wide message explaining the reasoning behind the policy without calling anyone out. *(Inform everyone)*
- **Seed data:** 55% A / 45% B
- **Research:** Conflict resolution (Fisher & Ury). Direct conversation prevents escalation. Class-wide messaging can feel passive-aggressive if the parent knows you saw the text.

### Scenario 6
**Context:** A parent asks you to give their child extra homework because they do not think the class is challenging enough.
- **A:** Offer enrichment options: "I hear you. Here are some extension activities [student] can choose from that go deeper into what we are learning." *(Meet the need differently)*
- **B:** Explain your homework philosophy: "Research shows that homework beyond [X] minutes has diminishing returns at this age. Here is how I build challenge into class instead." *(Educate the parent)*
- **Seed data:** 52% A / 48% B
- **Research:** Homework research (Cooper). Both approaches are valid. A gives the parent agency. B challenges an assumption but risks defensiveness.

### Scenario 7
**Context:** A parent whose child has an IEP emails you frustrated that accommodations are not being followed. They are right -- you missed one.
- **A:** Own it immediately: "You are right, and I apologize. I missed [specific accommodation]. Here is what I am doing to fix it starting tomorrow." *(Accountability)*
- **B:** Explain the context: "Thank you for flagging this. With [class size/schedule change], I need to rebuild my system for tracking accommodations. Here is my plan." *(Context then plan)*
- **Seed data:** 70% A / 30% B
- **Research:** Trust repair (Gottman). When you are wrong, own it fast. Explaining context before apologizing feels like excuse-making.

### Scenario 8
**Context:** A parent wants to be friends. They text you casually, invite you to social events, and share personal stories during pickup. You like them but need boundaries.
- **A:** Gently redirect: Keep conversations warm but school-focused. Do not respond to casual texts. Be friendly at pickup but do not extend conversations. *(Slow fade to professional)*
- **B:** Have an honest conversation: "I really enjoy talking with you. For [student's] sake, I want to keep our relationship professional so I can be objective and fair." *(Direct boundary)*
- **Seed data:** 65% A / 35% B
- **Research:** Professional boundaries (NASW adapted for education). The slow fade avoids an awkward conversation but can feel like rejection. Direct honesty is harder but clearer.

## 3. De-escalation -- Classroom Shuffle Scenarios

### Scenario 1
**Context:** A student flips their desk and is standing in the middle of the room breathing heavily. Other students are frozen.
**Question:** What is your first move?
- **A:** Calmly get the other students focused: "Everyone, eyes on me. Open to page 12." Then address the student. *(Triage the room first)*
- **B:** Address the student directly: "I see you are upset. I am here. Take a breath with me." *(Connect with the student first)*
- **C:** Call the office immediately for support. *(Get help)*
- **Correct: A** -- The room needs to be safe before you can help the student. 25 frozen students watching escalates everything. Once they are redirected, you can give the student your full attention.

### Scenario 2
**Context:** A student is refusing to do any work. Arms crossed, hood up, will not make eye contact. It has been 20 minutes.
**Question:** What do you do?
- **A:** Give a simple, low-pressure choice: "You can start with question 1 or question 5. Your call." *(Tiny choice, no lecture)*
- **B:** Write them a private note: "I notice you are having a hard time. No pressure. I am here when you are ready." *(Written, not verbal)*
- **C:** Tell them they will need to stay after to finish the work. *(Consequence)*
- **Correct: B** -- After 20 minutes, verbal approaches have failed. A written note changes the channel. It is private, non-confrontational, and gives them control over when to re-engage. A choice (option A) is good earlier but not after 20 minutes of shutdown.

### Scenario 3
**Context:** Two students are arguing loudly. It is escalating toward physical. The rest of the class is watching.
**Question:** What do you do?
- **A:** Step between them (not touching) and say firmly: "Both of you, separate. [Name], step to the door. [Name], sit down." *(Direct separation)*
- **B:** Call for backup from a neighboring teacher or admin while maintaining a calm presence. *(Get help first)*
- **C:** Address the class: "Everyone, hallway now." Remove the audience, then address the students. *(Remove the audience)*
- **Correct: A** -- Physical safety requires immediate action. Direct, calm, specific instructions with name usage cuts through the adrenaline. Getting the class out (C) takes too long when it is about to get physical. Calling for backup (B) is smart but not the FIRST move.

### Scenario 4
**Context:** A student says "I hate you" after you redirect their behavior in front of the class.
**Question:** How do you respond?
- **A:** Stay calm: "I hear you. We can talk about this when things cool down. Right now I need you to [simple redirect]." *(Absorb it, redirect)*
- **B:** Address it privately later: Say nothing in the moment, continue teaching, then check in at the end of class. *(Do not engage publicly)*
- **C:** Respond with empathy: "That sounds like you are really frustrated. I am not going anywhere. Let us figure this out." *(Name the emotion)*
- **Correct: A** -- You need to acknowledge without engaging. "I hear you" validates without agreeing or arguing. The redirect keeps the class moving. Private follow-up (B) is important but ignoring it completely in the moment can feel dismissive. C is good but can escalate if the student is not ready for empathy yet.

### Scenario 5
**Context:** A student is crying uncontrollably and cannot explain why. Other students are staring.
**Question:** What do you do?
- **A:** Quietly ask a trusted student to walk them to the counselor. *(Remove them from the spotlight)*
- **B:** Kneel next to them and say softly: "You do not have to talk. I am just going to sit here with you for a minute." *(Be present)*
- **C:** Give the class something to do, then sit with the student. *(Manage the room, then connect)*
- **Correct: C** -- The staring makes everything worse. Get the class working first (20 seconds), then give the student your full presence. Sending them to the counselor (A) can feel like you are getting rid of them. Sitting with them while 30 kids stare (B) is well-intentioned but adds pressure.

### Scenario 6
**Context:** A student throws a book across the room. Nobody is hit. The student immediately looks shocked at what they did.
**Question:** How do you respond?
- **A:** Ignore the throw for now. Walk to them calmly and say: "That looked like a big feeling. Let us take a walk." *(Prioritize the person, not the behavior)*
- **B:** Address the safety issue: "Books stay on desks. Are you okay? Let us talk outside." *(Name the boundary, then connect)*
- **C:** Have them pick up the book and apologize to the class. *(Accountability first)*
- **Correct: A** -- The student's shock shows they already know they made a mistake. Naming the feeling first builds trust. Making them apologize immediately (C) shames them in their most vulnerable moment. B is close but leading with the rule ("books stay on desks") can feel like a lecture when the student is already regulating.

### Scenario 7
**Context:** A student is verbally aggressive toward a para, calling them names and refusing to work with them. The lead teacher is across the room.
**Question (for the para):** What do you do?
- **A:** Stay calm, do not engage with the name-calling, and say: "I am going to give you some space. I will be right over here when you are ready." *(Disengage without abandoning)*
- **B:** Quietly signal the lead teacher for support. *(Get backup)*
- **C:** Firmly but calmly say: "I am here to help you. Those words are not okay. Let us start over." *(Set the boundary)*
- **Correct: A** -- As a para, your physical presence may be the trigger. Creating space while staying nearby shows you are not giving up on them. Signaling the teacher (B) is also appropriate but disengaging first prevents further escalation. Setting a boundary (C) is reasonable but in the moment of aggression, it can feel like a challenge.
